---
title: "Designing GeoScratch Around How Vision Already Works"
date: "2026-07-18"
---

Who hasn't stared at a maths problem and felt like it was written in a
foreign language? And who has felt the opposite: that moment where the
understanding clicks into place and the whole thing suddenly feels obvious?

I can't architect that second moment for everyone, in every mathematical
scenario. Intuitions about prime numbers, for instance, are hard-won and
mostly reserved for professional mathematicians. But there's one area where
humans already have an enormous head start: three dimensional space. We've
been navigating it, judging depth, and tracking objects moving through it
since before we could talk. That's intuition your visual system builds for
free, and it's the starting point for my honours research: how do we design
tools that put that same visual intuition to work for maths education,
instead of asking students to painfully rebuild it from formulas and
ambiguous static diagrams alone.

Textbook diagrams, lecture slides, notes on a whiteboard: these are all flat.
Every time you look at a page and try to work out which line is in front,
how far apart two points really are, or what a rotation actually looks like,
you're doing translation work the page isn't helping you with. Your brain is
used to real depth cues: shadows, lighting, occlusion. Take those away, and
even someone who understands the maths perfectly can misjudge the picture.
That's not a knowledge gap, it's a perception gap.

That's the question behind [GeoScratch](/projects): what happens if we
design 3D geometry teaching tools around how human vision actually works.
It's a block-based environment for learning 3D geometry and linear algebra,
and instead of static diagrams, it renders shapes using existing research on
visual perception: shadows that ground objects in space, occlusion
management that reveals what's in front of what.

![GeoScratch's block-based programming interface, with blocks for vector
arithmetic, planes, lines, and primitive shapes](/blog/geoscratch-blocks.png)

![A rendered scene grounding two objects in 3D space using shadows and
axis lines](/projects/geoscratch.png)

There's a second piece to it. Most tools show you the answer, a finished
vector or a completed projection, as a static result, without ever showing
you how it got there. GeoScratch animates the solution process itself,
letting students watch a rotation unfold or a projection form step by step
rather than just staring at where it ended up. Subtle motion and
highlighting also guide a student's attention to exactly the part of the
scene that matters most, the way an annotated distance calculation below
draws your eye straight to the value it's explaining.

![A GeoScratch scene annotating a point-to-plane distance calculation, with
labelled vectors P, Q, and the projection distance](/blog/geoscratch-distance-annotation.png)

The research itself tests which of these techniques actually help:
measuring how quickly and accurately people judge depth and orientation
under different rendering conditions, and whether watching a process
animate, rather than just seeing its result, helps them understand that
process more clearly. That part is still ongoing, so consider this post a
progress report rather than a conclusion.

For a lot of students, staring at a formula with no way in is the whole
problem. If we design tools that lead with what the eye already knows how to
do, before asking students to translate that into symbols, we close the gap
between the intuition a student already has and the page of formulas that's
supposed to describe it.
